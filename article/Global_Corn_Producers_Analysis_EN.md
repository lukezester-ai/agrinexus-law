# The Global Corn Map in May 2026: Who Produces, Who Exports, and Why the Price Just Moved on a Single Phone Call

*Analysis as of 19 May 2026*

---

## 1. The Big Picture: Who Grows the World's Corn

Corn (maize) is the largest grain crop in the world by volume — more than wheat or rice. Global production for the 2025/26 marketing year reached around **1.22 billion metric tonnes**, with five countries accounting for nearly 80% of it.

### The Top 5

| Rank | Country | Production 2025/26 (million tonnes) | Share of world output |
|:---:|:---|:---:|:---:|
| 1 | **United States** | ~385 | ~32% |
| 2 | **China** | ~295–300 | ~25% |
| 3 | **Brazil** | ~130 | ~11% |
| 4 | **European Union (27)** | ~62 | ~5% |
| 5 | **Argentina** | ~50 | ~4% |

Together these five produce about **77% of the world's corn**. The next ten producers (Ukraine, India, Mexico, South Africa, Russia, Canada, Indonesia, Nigeria, Ethiopia, the Philippines) make up most of the remainder.

### The Production Outlook for 2026/27 — Why It Matters Now

The 2026/27 forecast tells a dramatically different story:

- **China** is set to deliver a **record 307-million-tonne corn crop** in 2026/27, about 1% higher than a year ago. China is the world's second-largest corn producer behind the United States.
- **The United States** faces sharply rising risk. The same drought devastating Hard Red Winter wheat in Kansas, Oklahoma, and Texas threatens U.S. corn yields in the same belt. May 2026 export inspections came in at 51.3 million bushels, declining week-on-week — a warning sign.
- **Brazil** continues to grow as a dominant exporter, taking market share from the United States in Asia and the Middle East.
- **Argentina** had a record export year: exports raised 2.0 million tonnes to a record 18.0 million on robust December and January shipments and highly competitive export prices.
- **Ukraine** is still in the global picture despite the war, but exports are constrained by Black Sea logistics and Bosphorus congestion.

## 2. The United States: Still King, but Under Stress

The United States is the **largest producer, the largest consumer, and the largest exporter of corn in the world**. American farmers plant about 90 million acres of corn each year, with the majority of the crop grown in the Heartland — Iowa, Illinois, Nebraska, Minnesota, Indiana, Kansas, South Dakota, Ohio, Missouri, and Wisconsin. Iowa alone produces nearly 20% of the entire U.S. crop.

Most of the corn is used domestically — as the main energy ingredient in livestock feed and for fuel ethanol production. The U.S. exports between 10–20% of its corn production, with the largest international markets being **Mexico, China, Japan, and Colombia**.

### The 2026 Stress Points

Three factors are putting U.S. corn under unprecedented pressure simultaneously:

**1. The Iran-driven fertilizer shock.** Anhydrous ammonia prices climbed from $828/ton in late 2024 to $1,123/ton by mid-April 2026. Nitrogen solutions rose 25%. This directly raises U.S. corn production costs.

**2. The Midwest weather pattern.** Drought has hit the southern Plains hard. While the Corn Belt proper (Iowa, Illinois) is less affected, marginal acres in Kansas and southern Nebraska are under stress.

**3. The Trump-China trade dance.** This is the dominant factor in pricing right now — see Section 4.

The U.S. corn outlook for 2025/26 has been adjusted: exports were raised by 100 million bushels to 3.3 billion, while ending stocks were cut by 100 million bushels to 2.1 billion. **Stocks are tightening just as production faces headwinds — a classically bullish setup that has been only partially reflected in prices.**

---

## 3. China: The Quiet Giant That Reshapes the Market

China is by far the largest single consumer of corn in the world, and its production is rising fast. Several forces drive this:

**Food security policy.** Beijing learned from 2020-2022 that supply chain dependence is dangerous. The government has invested heavily in domestic corn production, agricultural inputs, and grain reserves.

**The record 307-million-tonne 2026/27 crop.** Strong growth in poultry meat production and stable demand from the swine sector and domestic processing support a 4.0-million-tonne growth in corn consumption.

**Sharply reduced imports.** Chinese corn imports are forecast at only 6.0 million tonnes for 2026/27 — well below substantially larger volumes in recent years. In 2020-2022, China was importing 20-25 million tonnes; that has now collapsed.

### Why This Matters for the World

When China imports less, two things happen simultaneously:

1. **Global prices come under pressure** — China's bid disappears from the market.
2. **U.S., Brazilian, and Ukrainian exporters lose their biggest growth market** — and have to find buyers elsewhere (Mexico, Egypt, Southeast Asia).

This puts **Brazil and the United States in direct competition** for the Middle East, Africa, and Southeast Asian markets that previously absorbed surplus capacity heading to China.

## 4. The Price Story: How $4.26 Became $4.76 in Four Months

This is the most fascinating chapter of the 2026 corn story. Corn prices on the Chicago Board of Trade have moved in a tight but politically driven range.

### The Timeline

- **13 January 2026**: May 2026 CBOT corn hit a low of $4.26¼ per bushel after a bearish January WASDE report. U.S. 2025 corn harvested acres rose to 91.3 million, up 1.3 million acres. Corn acres planted reached 98.8 million, up 100,000 acres. U.S. corn yield increased a half bushel to 186.5 bushels per acre. Production, yield, and harvested acres all increased.
- **Early February 2026**: prices stabilized around $4.42 — up 15¾ cents from the low but still struggling to rally. The December 2026 corn contract averaged $4.59.
- **12 May 2026**: the bullish WASDE report showed U.S. wheat collapsing to a 50-year low. Corn was less affected but still benefited from grain-complex strength.
- **14-15 May 2026**: corn briefly reached around $4.87, the highest in a year, on Chinese buying speculation.
- **18 May 2026**: corn jumped back above $4.70 per bushel after the U.S. signaled potential large-scale Chinese purchases.
- **19 May 2026**: corn slipped to around $4.70 per bushel, retreating from the recent surge after long-awaited details emerged regarding China's commitment to purchase U.S. agricultural goods. China pledged to buy at least $17 billion annually in U.S. agricultural goods through 2028 after high-level talks in Beijing between President Donald Trump and President Xi Jinping aimed at easing trade tensions.

### The Anatomy of the Move

In other words: over four months, corn climbed approximately **12-15%** based not on supply fundamentals but on a single thesis — *China will buy*. And as soon as China's Ministry of Commerce stated that the two countries had merely established a "guiding target" to increase agricultural trade, without mentioning the $17 billion figure, prices retreated.

**The market is reading politics, not crops.**

This is the typical pattern in modern commodity markets: a small headline can move billions of dollars of futures value within hours. In May 2026, a single phone call between Trump and Xi moved corn prices by 3-4% in a single trading session. After Trump's meeting with President Xi last year, China had initially committed to buying 12 million metric tonnes of soybeans, alongside a broader pledge to purchase 25 million tonnes annually for three years.

### What's Pinning Prices Up

Despite the volatility, two structural forces are keeping corn from going lower:

1. **The fertilizer shock from the Iran war.** Production costs for the 2026 crop are 25-35% higher than for 2025. Farmers must receive higher prices to plant.
2. **The Wheat-Corn link.** When U.S. wheat collapses (smallest crop since 1972/73), feed users (poultry, cattle, hogs) substitute more corn into rations. This raises corn demand by 5-10 million tonnes globally.

### What's Pinning Prices Down

Two forces hold corn back from a true rally:

1. **The record Chinese crop.** A 307 million tonne Chinese harvest means much less Chinese import demand.
2. **Argentina and Brazil.** Both are selling aggressively and stealing market share.

## 5. Export Rankings: Who Sells, Who Buys

Production tells one story; **export power** tells another. Here is the export ranking for 2025/26:

| Rank | Country | Exports (million tonnes) | Note |
|:---:|:---|:---:|:---|
| 1 | **United States** | ~63 | About 38% of world corn exports |
| 2 | **Brazil** | ~45-50 | Growing fastest; now dominant in Asia |
| 3 | **Argentina** | ~18 (record) | Most price-competitive |
| 4 | **Ukraine** | ~20-25 | Despite the war, still relevant |
| 5 | **European Union** | ~5-7 | Mostly internal |

The largest buyers, by contrast:

- **Mexico** (the largest single buyer of U.S. corn)
- **Japan** (the largest premium-grade buyer)
- **Egypt** (3-4 million tonnes a year for feed)
- **South Korea, Vietnam, Iran, Colombia, the Philippines**

### A Key 2026 Detail

Iran is among countries with higher corn imports for 2025/26 despite the war. This is one of the paradoxes of 2026: Iran is blocking Hormuz to oil exports, but its own poultry and dairy sectors still need imported feed grains. Corn imports to Iran are higher even as the country tries to weaponize the Persian Gulf.

## 6. The Big Producers in Detail

### Brazil — The Rising Star

Brazil is the great success story of the past decade. Its "safrinha" (second-crop) corn, planted after soybeans, has revolutionized supply. Today Brazil is competitive with the U.S. on freight to Asia and a major supplier to the Middle East. Brazilian corn now arrives in Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and Iran at prices the U.S. cannot match.

### Argentina — The Comeback

After years of political turmoil and currency crises, Argentina is back. Argentina's corn exports for 2025/26 reached a record 18.0 million tonnes, supported by competitive prices and a strong December-January shipment window. Milei's economic reforms have unlocked dollar flows and made Argentine farmers more responsive to global prices.

### Ukraine — Wounded but Functional

Even with the war, Ukraine still exports 20-25 million tonnes of corn a year. Most of it now goes by Black Sea (despite Russian attacks and the Bosphorus bottleneck) or overland through Romania and Poland. Ukrainian corn is the cheapest in Europe and crucial for European feed mills.

### European Union — Marginal Player

The EU produces around 62 million tonnes (a slight increase versus 2024/25), but it imports almost as much. Internal flows from Romania, France, Bulgaria, and Hungary feed the rest of the bloc.

### India, Mexico, South Africa, Indonesia — The Domestic Giants

These countries produce 15-40 million tonnes each but consume almost all of it internally. They are not export players, but their domestic balance directly affects whether they need to import.

## 7. Where Corn Prices Go Next — Three Scenarios

For traders, farmers, and analysts, the question is: what happens in the next six months?

### Scenario A — China Confirms Its Purchases (probability 40%)

If Beijing officially signs the $17 billion deal and starts loading U.S. corn ships in June-July, prices could climb above $5.00/bushel by late summer 2026. Brazilian and Argentine corn would still compete, but U.S. farmers would have a clear demand pillar.

### Scenario B — China Backs Off (probability 35%)

If China's Ministry of Commerce continues to say "guiding target" instead of confirming the $17 billion figure, corn drifts back to $4.30-4.50/bushel through the harvest. American farmers face their second consecutive year of falling income, despite record-high fertilizer costs.

### Scenario C — U.S. Drought Worsens (probability 25%)

If the Plains drought spreads to Iowa and Illinois (currently it has not), and U.S. corn yields fall below 175 bushels/acre (versus 186.5 in 2025), prices could break $5.50/bushel regardless of the China deal. This is a tail risk that markets are not yet pricing in.

## Conclusion: Corn as the Mirror of 2026

Corn in May 2026 reflects everything happening in the world: the Iran war (through fertilizer costs), the U.S.-China trade dance (through demand expectations), the Bosphorus and Black Sea (through Ukrainian and Russian flows), Trump's tariffs (through farmer profitability), and the Brazilian-Argentine rise (through competition for export markets).

**For the global feed industry**, corn is becoming structurally more expensive. Even when prices look "low" in dollar terms ($4.70/bushel is not historically high), the cost-to-produce floor has risen sharply, meaning prices cannot fall as much as in past cycles.

**For Bulgarian and Eastern European farmers**, this is a moment of opportunity. Their corn is cheap on the global market (Black Sea-adjusted), wheat is in deficit, and European feed mills need supply. The 2026 harvest should be very profitable if the weather cooperates.

**For consumers**, this means that animal protein prices (chicken, pork, eggs, dairy) will stay elevated through 2026 and into 2027. Corn is the feed of feeds — when it stays high, everything in the supermarket meat aisle stays high.

The corn market in 2026 is no longer just an agricultural market. It is a geopolitical thermometer — and right now, it is reading hot.
